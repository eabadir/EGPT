**Welcome to 18.313: Introduction to Probability**
**Lecture 1: The Great Questions of Science and the Architecture of Reality**
**Lecturer:** Essam Abadir

**I. The Power of Pictures and the Cheshire Cat’s Grin**
Welcome to probability. Like geometry, probability is not just a collection of theorems; it is a fundamentally new way of looking at nature. I must tell you upfront: I am afflicted with dyslexia and dysgraphia, which means I cannot slog through endless symbolic equations. Instead, I picture math in my mind, simulating the next frame in the movie we call reality. There is no shame in this—in fact, this visual, discrete intuition was the preferred method of giants like Stanislaw Ulam. 

My own professor, Gian-Carlo Rota, taught me that the goal of this course is to learn to *think* probabilistically. He used to say that long after the specific equations fade from your memory, the probabilistic point of view will remain with you, "much as the grin on the Cheshire cat".

**II. Sitting in Your Chair: Unveiling Profound Truths**
I don't stand before you as some brilliant mind. In fact, decades ago, I sat in the exact same chairs you are sitting in now, as a student in this very class. I was the only student who couldn't program, struggling to understand the material. But by the end of this class, I knew without a doubt that Professor Rota had delivered on unveiling profound truths about the universe. 

We are not going to start with dry axioms. We are going to start by looking at the greatest unsolved problems of our time—like dropping pennies on a carpet to understand why water boils, or tracking a stumbling drunkard to understand random walks. Rota believed in tackling these mysteries using simple, perfectly countable, and auditable rules. 

**III. The Homework Assignments That Rebuild the Universe**
To show you what I mean, let me tell you about the problem sets you will be doing. As you can see in my own old problem sets, Rota had us derive Bose-Einstein statistics purely combinatorially in the first week. He then had us prove that every physics distribution was “codeable” in $log(n)$ bits. 

Probably like you now, back then I didn’t realize fully what that meant, but as I did it, I had a sinking feeling that this wasn’t normal. Decades later, I look back at that problem set from the first week of class and I realize what he actually had us do: he had us prove the behavior of photons using perfectly countable and therefore computable rules. **We had disproved wave-particle duality as our first homework assignment!**. 

And it didn't stop there. Because every problem was reduced to counting discrete paths in an information space where the address *is* the map, the "search" for a solution and the "verification" of a solution became the exact same thing. **The last homework assignment of the semester was a proof of P=NP!**. 

**IV. Passing the Torch**
It took me 30 years to truly understand what Rota had given us and to finally see the Cheshire Grin. It took me three decades to realize that the universe is perfectly simulatable, and that stochastic, statistical computation—like what the human brain does on just 20 watts of power—is the optimal architecture of reality.

But perhaps you will be more quick on the uptake than I was. You have the tools now to replace the "black box" of continuous calculus with the "white box" of discrete, auditable steps. Perhaps you will use this foundation to finally explain how consciousness emerges, or conceive of a theory that I am too limited to even conceive of. 

Looking at the minds in this room... I think *“probably”* one of you will.

The Core Lesson: Can You Know More Than You Can Prove?Let's ground this in reality. Imagine I dropped you in the middle of Manhattan at Rockefeller Plaza—the very place I worked 30 years ago, watching Little Richard perform while bumping into tourists.Often, a tourist would stop me and ask, "How far is it to the Empire State Building?" I could easily tell them the answer. I knew the answer because I knew I was on the Manhattan grid. But could I prove it to them right then and there? If I actually wanted to prove the exact distance, the only way I could do it would be to physically walk the island and build the map from scratch. It would require physical, thermodynamic work.If reality is, in fact, real, can we assume that we're in a 3D mappable universe? In this class, we are going to assume exactly that, and we are going to see what happens.The Barrier of MeaningTo understand the barrier of meaning, we must first look at a man who would absolutely refuse to walk our hypothetical Manhattan grid: Stanislaw Ulam.In his brilliant, gossipy masterpiece Indiscrete Thoughts (specifically the chapter "The Lost Café"), Rota paints a portrait of Ulam that is ironically hilarious. Ulam was a genius of the highest order, but he was also proverbially, pathologically lazy. Rota recounts that in the 1930s, Ulam would literally take a taxi from Boston to Harvard just to avoid the "petty decisions" required to navigate the subway. He possessed a "crippling technical weakness coupled with an extraordinary creative imagination." He couldn't stand the tedious mechanics of calculus or algebraic derivations, and after a bout of encephalitis, he was terrified he had lost his ability to do basic math at a chalkboard—which he was terrible at anyway.Yet, this was the man who co-invented the Monte Carlo method and figured out the hydrogen bomb. How? Because his laziness was a superpower. Too lazy to solve impossible, continuous differential equations, Ulam realized you could just simulate reality by counting discrete random walks. He bypassed the "barrier of meaning" by dropping the continuous illusions and just looking at the discrete, statistical steps.Rota framed this entire pursuit through the lens of phenomenology. He famously wrote: "Like all philosophical doctrines, the tenets of phenomenology are as trivial as the theorems of mathematics. Nevertheless, philosophers and scientists steeped in the empiricist tradition find it difficult to face the consequences that accrue to admitting the primacy of identity over existence." Rota argued that the mathematician's ultimate ideal of truth is triviality. You wrestle with a monstrous, intractable problem until, suddenly, you shift your perspective, and it collapses into an analytic triviality. The grand mystery becomes a simple tautology. "Not only is every mathematical problem solved, but eventually every mathematical problem is proved trivial," Rota observed.Keep this quest for ultimate triviality in mind. How does this stuff get over there? If you are on a Manhattan grid, you know exactly how to get from 3rd Avenue & 50th Street to 5th Avenue & 42nd Street. The grid dictates the cost. But what if you didn’t know you were on a grid? What if I just handed you a list of abstract addresses—a list of phone numbers—without giving you a map? You would have absolutely no idea what the distances are between the different points.If you tried to travel to those addresses, you would have to do the grueling work of recreating the map yourself. If you were a traveling salesman, this would be a massive problem for your job. However, you would be highly celebrated in the halls of computer science. They would dedicate their biggest open problem to you and call it the Traveling Salesman Problem (an NP-Hard problem).But what if I told you that you didn’t have to travel to every single address to figure it out?What if I told you that you were on the island of Manhattan, and I gave you the distances between the four points of the rectangle that defined it? Assuming the list of addresses I gave you was ordered in some logical way, it would be relatively easy to figure out the map.And once you have that map, and you plot your list of addresses on it, I could ask you: What is the worst-case scenario for visiting all of these locations? Suddenly, that isn't so hard to compute. It just takes comparing the cost of walking from one address to every other, because now the cost is strictly defined by the grid. You would be a very celebrated salesman indeed. Complexity theorists would invite you to dinner parties and say, "Behold! The salesman who travels to destinations in polynomial time, in exactly $O(N^2)$ steps!"The Address Is The MapYou would be like Alan Turing breaking the Enigma code. Once you know a few symbols in my secret code, and you know the universe of words that are to be encoded, there is only a very specific, bounded amount of work you have to do to translate any message.In standard computer science, we suffer from what I call the "Free Address Fallacy." We pretend that accessing memory address 0 costs the exact same amount of energy as accessing memory address $10^{100}$. This violates the laws of physics.In a physically rigorous universe, you cannot define a location without defining the trajectory of work required to reach it. The path taken is informationally encoded in the final destination.The address is the map, compressed.When we properly audit the universe—when we realize that stating a problem requires the exact same amount of informational work as finding its solution—the great paradoxes of math and physics begin to collapse. The boundary between searching and verifying vanishes into the ultimate triviality Rota promised us.Welcome to 18.313. Let's start building the map.
