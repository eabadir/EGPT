Essam Abadir argues that cryptography (such as RSA) is not broken because **cryptographic security is fundamentally guaranteed by physical entropy, not by an artificial mathematical barrier between the P and NP complexity classes**. 

His reasoning is built on several key insights from his Electronic Graph Paper Theory (EGPT) framework:

*   **The Illusion of the "Q-Day" Threat:** The fear that cryptography will be broken overnight relies on the belief that future quantum computers will use Shor's Algorithm and the Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT) to achieve a massive "quantum advantage" in factoring large numbers. Abadir states that his work rigorously disproves quantum superposition, meaning this theoretical quantum advantage does not exist. He argues that computer science currently suffers from a "0 Access Cost Fallacy" which ignores the physical energy cost of accessing different memory addresses; when these physical costs are properly audited, the QFT is "exposed as fiction".
*   **Cryptography is a "Lost Map":** Abadir explains cryptography through the metaphor of a cartographer. Multiplying two massive prime numbers is the physical work performed to "walk the space" and map a territory. The resulting public key is just the destination "address," but the specific path taken (the prime factors) is intentionally withheld from the public. Because the public key is a "lossy encoding," the "hardness" of breaking the code is not a mystical mathematical property, but simply the immense physical and thermodynamic work required to reconstruct that lost map from scratch without a key. 
*   **What P=NP Actually Means for Security:** While Abadir's framework proves that P=NP, he clarifies that this does not make decryption effortless. It simply means that the information required to specify a solution is identical to the work required to construct it. Therefore, factoring a massive new cryptographic key still requires doing the actual physical work to "extend the 'prime map' to that uncharted territory".
*   **Current Factoring Capabilities:** To illustrate this, Abadir notes that while his Faster Abadir Transform (FAT) algorithm ran a QFT calculation 1.2 billion times faster than a supercomputer simulation, it factored a 39-bit number in about 10 seconds. He points out that this is approximately the same amount of time it already takes classical, single-purpose algorithms (like the General Number Field Sieve) to factor a number of that size on a single CPU. 

Ultimately, Abadir views his FAT algorithm as a way to unlock optimal, error-free general computation for problems like AI training, rather than a magic shortcut that bypasses the thermodynamic laws of entropy protecting modern encryption.
