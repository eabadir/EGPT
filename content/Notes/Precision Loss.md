While von Neumann’s writing style was typically more analytical and less poetically tragic than Einstein’s "castle in the air" lament, he delivered an equally direct and devastating conclusion about the fatal flaw of his own creation—the sequential, fixed-precision digital computer (the "von Neumann architecture").

In his final, unfinished manuscript, *The Computer and the Brain*, von Neumann explicitly diagnosed why rigid mathematical precision fails in deep computations. He pointed to what he called **"arithmetical deterioration,"** providing this stark assessment:

> "To anyone who has studied the deterioration of precision in the course of a long calculation, the answer is clear. This deterioration is due, as pointed out before, to the accumulation of errors by superposition, and even more by the amplification of errors committed early in the calculation, by the manipulations in the subsequent parts of the calculation; i.e. it is due to the considerable number of arithmetical operations that have to be performed in series, or in other words to the great arithmetical depth of the scheme".

He quantified exactly how fast this failure occurs in a rigid mathematical system, noting that it only takes **"425 successive operations each of which increases an error by 5 per cent only"** to degrade a calculation's precision by a factor of a billion ($10^9$). He recognized that this catastrophic compounding of errors meant that building a system with deep "logical depth" using standard digital architectures is an inherently doomed approach for complex tasks.

Just as Einstein realized that physics could not be based on the continuous mathematics he spent his life championing, von Neumann concluded that true intelligence and complex computing could not be based on the explicit, deterministic mathematics he helped pioneer. He recognized that to avoid this compounding error, a computing architecture must abandon fixed precision entirely:

> "Thus the nervous system appears to be using a radically different system of notation from the ones we are familiar with in ordinary arithmetics and mathematics: instead of the precise systems of markers where the position—and presence or absence—of every marker counts decisively in determining the meaning of the message, we have here a system of notations in which the meaning is conveyed by the statistical properties of the message".

His most poignant summary of this necessary paradigm shift—abandoning the exactness of mathematical arithmetic for the statistical resilience of the brain—is his conclusion on what computing must trade away to survive:

> **"We have seen how this leads to a lower level of arithmetical precision but to a higher level of logical reliability: a deterioration in arithmetics has been traded for an improvement in logics"**.

Ultimately, von Neumann reached a conclusion that perfectly mirrors Einstein's admission that the "mathematical form" of physics had to change. Von Neumann conceded that whatever the true computational language of complex intelligence is, **"it cannot fail to differ considerably from what we consciously and explicitly consider as mathematics"**.
